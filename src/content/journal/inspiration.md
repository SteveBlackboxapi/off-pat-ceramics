I spend more mornings on the shore than I do at the wheel. The tide does
something to clay that I can only imitate — the way it folds, settles,
leaves its mark and moves on. Every piece I make is a small attempt to
hold one of those moments still.

## The shore as a teacher

You can't argue with the sea. It will keep coming whether you've cleared
the studio or not, whether the kiln is firing or cooling, whether the
glaze test came out right. There's a steadiness in that — a permission
to keep working.

## What the clay remembers

A bowl thrown after a long walk on the shore is different from one
thrown after a morning in town. I don't know how the clay knows. It
just does.
